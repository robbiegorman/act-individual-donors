Purpose

Why this matters for Austin Coming Together

ACT’s individual donor system should do more than track gifts. It should help the organization understand who is connected to the work, what parts of the Austin community and ACT’s mission they care about, how they first became engaged, and what kind of relationship-building or stewardship should happen next.

Honor ACT’s relationships

Track the people, partners, residents, neighbors, volunteers, board members, and supporters connected to ACT’s work.

Support strategic fundraising

Move from disconnected donor records toward a clear system for cultivation, stewardship, and individual giving growth.

Connect gifts to mission

Understand which initiatives, campaigns, events, or community priorities are motivating donors to support ACT.

Strengthen finance alignment

Keep Salesforce focused on donor relationships while ensuring gifts, restrictions, deposits, and revenue categories reconcile with QuickBooks.

Current snapshot

Where ACT appears to be in developing the system

This page is intended to help ACT and the data team translate individual donor strategy into a practical Salesforce design. It should be refined as current exports, spreadsheets, QuickBooks history, donation platform data, and email marketing lists are reviewed.

Current-state assumptions

  • ACT likely has donor and supporter information spread across multiple places: QuickBooks, spreadsheets, donation tools, email lists, events, and staff relationships.
  • QuickBooks may contain useful giving history, but it should not become ACT’s primary donor relationship system.
  • Salesforce should help ACT build a more intentional individual giving infrastructure, not simply recreate old spreadsheets.
  • ACT’s system should account for the reality that donors may also be community partners, residents, volunteers, board connections, event attendees, or initiative champions.

Salesforce owns the relationship

Donor identity, ACT connection, cultivation strategy, stewardship, campaign history, interest areas, and next actions.

QuickBooks owns accounting truth

Deposits, revenue classification, reconciliation, transaction records, fund restrictions, and financial reporting.

Email tools support communication

Subscription status, email engagement, audience segments, campaign tags, and donor communications.

Donation platforms feed activity

Online gifts, recurring donations, payment processor IDs, donor-entered details, and campaign forms.

Design principles

What each system should and should not do

ACT’s Salesforce system should help staff understand and grow relationships. QuickBooks should remain the financial source of truth. This keeps the donor system useful without turning accounting software into a second CRM.

Salesforce should answer: “Who is connected to ACT, why do they care about the Austin community, and what should happen next in the relationship?”
QuickBooks should answer: “Was the money received, classified correctly, deposited correctly, and reconciled?”
Data flow

Recommended system architecture for ACT

The system should allow online and offline gifts to move through a consistent path while preserving clear ownership of donor relationship data and accounting data.

1

Supporter activity

Online gift, check, event attendance, board referral, volunteer interest, partner connection, or community engagement.

2

Salesforce record

Donor identity, ACT connection, initiative interest, campaign, gift, relationship owner, and stewardship workflow.

3

QuickBooks reconciliation

Transaction amount, revenue category, fund or restriction, deposit batch, and accounting record.

4

Relationship loop

Thank-you, impact update, event invitation, volunteer opportunity, campaign follow-up, or next donor conversation.

Implementation phases

Phase 1 versus future build

ACT does not need to build everything at once. Phase 1 should focus on clean donor records, gift history, stewardship, and QuickBooks alignment. Future phases can add deeper cultivation strategy and donor insight.

Reporting use cases

The fields should help ACT answer better questions

A field is only valuable if it supports a decision, process, workflow, or report. These reporting use cases can help ACT and the data team evaluate which fields are essential.

Fundraising

  • New individual donors this year
  • Lapsed donors needing re-engagement
  • Recurring donor growth
  • Major donor prospects
  • Giving by campaign or appeal

Finance

  • Gifts by fund or restriction
  • Deposits needing review
  • Missing QuickBooks IDs
  • Tax-deductible totals
  • Revenue category checks

Community engagement

  • Donors by ACT connection type
  • Donors by Austin community connection
  • Event attendees who later gave
  • Volunteers who became donors
  • Partner-connected supporters

Leadership

  • Individual giving pipeline
  • Donor retention
  • Revenue forecast
  • Stewardship activity
  • Support by initiative area
